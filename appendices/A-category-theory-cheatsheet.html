13  Category Theory Cheatsheet

A quick reference for the categorical concepts that appear in this tutorial. Each entry gives a one-sentence definition, where it shows up in panproto, and a pointer to the literature.

13.1 Category

A collection of objects and morphisms (arrows between objects) equipped with composition and identities, satisfying associativity and unit laws.

In panproto: The category GAT has theories as objects and theory morphisms as arrows. The category Mod(T) has models of a theory \(T\) as objects and model homomorphisms as arrows. See (Mac Lane 1998, Ch. 1).

13.2 Functor

A structure-preserving map between categories: it sends objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms, preserving composition and identities.

In panproto: A theory morphism \(F: T_1 \to T_2\) induces a functor \(F^*: \mathbf{Mod}(T_2) \to \mathbf{Mod}(T_1)\) that pulls back models. This is the restrict operation—data migration in the reverse direction. See (Mac Lane 1998, Ch. 1).

13.3 Natural Transformation

A family of morphisms between two functors that commutes with the functors’ action on morphisms.

In panproto: Natural transformations between migration functors correspond to systematic ways of transforming one migration strategy into another. They ensure that different migration paths yield compatible results. See (Mac Lane 1998, Ch. 2).

13.4 Limit

A universal cone over a diagram—the “most general” object that maps into every object in the diagram compatibly.

In panproto: Products (a special case of limits) appear when combining independent schemas. The limit construction ensures no information is lost. See (Mac Lane 1998, Ch. 5).

13.5 Colimit

A universal cocone under a diagram—the “most general” object that every object in the diagram maps into, gluing shared parts together.

In panproto: Colimits of theories are how new protocols are defined. The pushout (a colimit of a span) merges two theories along a shared base. See (Mac Lane 1998, Ch. 5) and (Adámek and Rosický 1994, Ch. 11).

13.6 Pushout

The colimit of a span \(A \leftarrow S \rightarrow B\): the universal object that glues \(A\) and \(B\) together along their shared sub-object \(S\).

In panproto: colimit(t1, t2, shared) computes a pushout of theories. This is the primary mechanism for composing component theories into protocols. See (Barr and Wells 1990, Ch. 9).

13.7 Adjunction

A pair of functors \(F \dashv G\) between two categories such that \(\mathrm{Hom}(FA, B) \cong \mathrm{Hom}(A, GB)\) naturally. The left adjoint \(F\) freely constructs; the right adjoint \(G\) forgets structure.

In panproto: The migration functors \(\Sigma_F \dashv \Delta_F \dashv \Pi_F\) form adjoint triples. \(\Delta_F\) (restrict) is the pullback; \(\Sigma_F\) (left push) and \(\Pi_F\) (right push) are its adjoints. These adjunctions guarantee the round-trip properties of lenses. See (Spivak 2012) and (Mac Lane 1998, Ch. 4).

13.8 Kan Extension

The universal way to extend a functor along another functor. Left and right Kan extensions generalize colimits and limits, respectively.

In panproto: The left and right pushforward operations (\(\Sigma_F\) and \(\Pi_F\)) are computed as left and right Kan extensions along the theory morphism \(F\). See (Mac Lane 1998, Ch. 10) and (Spivak 2012).

13.9 Fibration

A functor \(p: \mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{B}\) with a lifting property: for every morphism in the base and object in the fiber above its codomain, there is a canonical “cartesian” lift.

In panproto: The projection from the category of all schemas (across all protocols) down to the category of theories is a fibration. Lifting along this fibration is what lets panproto migrate schemas when the underlying protocol theory changes. See (Barr and Wells 1990, Ch. 12).

13.10 Initial Algebra

The least fixed point of an endofunctor: an algebra \(F(A) \to A\) such that every other \(F\)-algebra receives a unique homomorphism from it.

In panproto: W-types are initial algebras of polynomial functors. Recursive data structures (trees, nested records, lists) are modeled as W-types, and their migrations are derived from the universal property of initiality. See (Adámek and Rosický 1994, Ch. 10) and (Gambino and Hyland 2004).

13.11 Polynomial Functor

An endofunctor built from sums, products, and exponentiation by a fixed set: \(P(X) = \sum_{a:A} X^{B(a)}\). Polynomial functors classify branching structures.

In panproto: Each schema determines a polynomial functor whose initial algebra is the type of well-founded data conforming to that schema. Migrations between schemas lift to maps between the corresponding polynomial functors. See (Gambino and Hyland 2004) and (Abbott et al. 2005).

Abbott, Michael, Thorsten Altenkirch, and Neil Ghani. 2005. “Containers: Constructing Strictly Positive Types.” Theoretical Computer Science 342 (1): 3–27. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tcs.2005.06.002.
Adámek, Jiří, and Jiří Rosický. 1994. Locally Presentable and Accessible Categories. Vol. 189. London Mathematical Society Lecture Note Series. Cambridge University Press. https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511600579.
Barr, Michael, and Charles Wells. 1990. Category Theory for Computing Science. Prentice Hall.
Gambino, Nicola, and J. Martin E. Hyland. 2004. “Wellfounded Trees and Dependent Polynomial Functors.” Types for Proofs and Programs (TYPES 2003), Lecture notes in computer science, vol. 3085: 210–25. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-540-24849-1_14.
Mac Lane, Saunders. 1998. Categories for the Working Mathematician. 2nd ed. Vol. 5. Graduate Texts in Mathematics. Springer. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4757-4721-8.
Spivak, David I. 2012. “Functorial Data Migration.” Information and Computation 217: 31–51. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ic.2012.05.001.

# Category Theory Cheatsheet {.appendix}

**In panproto**: The category **GAT** has theories as objects and theory morphisms as arrows. The category **Mod(T)** has models of a theory $T$ as objects and model homomorphisms as arrows. See [@maclane1998, Ch. 1].
